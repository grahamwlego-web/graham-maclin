How a Raccoon Attack
Became a Story

By Graham Maclin  ·  February 28, 2026

Fourteen pairs of glowing eyes. That's the image that started it all. I kept thinking — what would it actually feel like to be outside at night, locked out of your house, and suddenly realize you are completely surrounded?

That's where Terror in the Dark came from. Not from a nightmare, not from a movie — just from asking "what's the worst thing that could happen on an ordinary night?"

Bob and Josh

I wanted two regular kids. Not superheroes, not chosen ones — just two eleven year olds having a sleepover in Miami, eating popcorn and Sour Patch Kids, watching a movie. The most normal night possible. And then everything goes wrong.

The locked door was the key moment. Once they can't get back inside, they have no shelter, no phone, no adults. They only have each other. That's when the story really starts.

"What's the worst thing that could happen on an ordinary night?"

The raccoons

People always laugh when I say raccoons are the villains. But think about it — raccoons are fast, they travel in groups, they're not afraid of people, and they come out at night. They're the perfect threat for a story like this.

The zipline, the creek, Old Alfred's mansion, the shed — all of it came from asking one question over and over: where would two scared kids run next?

The epilogue

My favorite part of the whole book is the epilogue. Bob walking home from basketball, stopping outside his house, and just thinking — this is where it happened. That moment of looking back at something scary and realizing you survived it. That felt important to include.

Also, Josh broke his ankle again after slipping on his sister's roller skate. Some people just have bad luck.

Check your locks tonight. You never know.
